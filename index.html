About Me

I’m a second year Ph.D. student in Computer Science at Purdue University, Indianapolis working in machine learning and computer visualization under the direction of Dr. Mukhopadhyay and Dr. Fang. My work focuses on developing new methods in adaptive machine learning to better understand the environment and integrate human feedback into the system.

Thesis: Interactive Machine Learning - A Multimodal Approach

The goal is to drive advances in adaptive machine learning by predicting successful metrics in educational use cases. Using natural language processing, extraction of audio video features, and active learning recommendations, the algorithm bridges the gap between human centric and machine centric approaches.

Research Interests:

My research interests are in machine learning, natural language processing, adversarial robustness in social networks and data ethics.

1. Brain-based Indicators of Passenger Trust During Open-Road Driving. Published in 2021 IEEE 94th Vehicular Technology Conference-VTC2021-Fall. IEEE.
DSouza, K. M., Dang, T., Metcalfe, J. S., & Bhattacharya, S. (2021, September).
A novel approach to correlating the passenger-driver trust levels can be elicited from brain-based indicators that are extracted from timelocked electroencephalogram (EEG) signals that capture both sensory and cognitive processes. In this paper, we propose a trust identification technique utilizing evoked response potential (ERP) events with beta/alpha frequency ratios from road events. The study strengthens the existence of a cognitive correlation between passenger-driver behavior and thus improve the efficiency of driver safety systems.

2. Title: Social Media and Fake News Detection using Adversarial Collaboration. Published in Proceedings of the 55th Hawaii International Conference on System Sciences.
DSouza, K. M., & French, A. M. (2022, January).
Adversarial attacks target creating misinformation in online information systems. This behavior can be viewed as an instrument to manipulate the online social media networks for cultural, social, economic, and political gains. A closer look at the mechanisms of implementing adversarial attacks in social media systems helps build robust intelligent systems that can withstand future vulnerabilities.

Workshops Presented:

1. Title: Data Ethics Practices with Machine Learning and AI
Women in Data Science, Greater Lafayette, Purdue University, March 7-8, 2022. Affiliated to Women in Data Science, Stanford University
Data ethics encompasses the ethical topics that surround the use of data. Machine learning in AI applications has improved our lives. However, problems of bias, fairness and privacy unknowingly creep in after deployment. The presentation discusses data ethics considerations in the real world, tools available to mitigate the risk from data bias and a demonstration of bias in a dataset.
Workshop

2. Title: Can Artificial Intelligence (AI) be used to monitor and enhance cPLTL workshops?
2022 Biennial Conference on Chemical Education, Purdue University, July 31, 2022.
There is a lack of AI-supported tools that assist the instructor in providing feedback of progression of learning – collaboration, effectiveness, participation. This preliminary study demonstrates that AI-Enabled models such as Support Vector Regression and Neural Networks have the potential to monitor real-time cPLTL session data for session effectiveness.
